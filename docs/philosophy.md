# Why This System Works (And Why Most Don't)

Most personal knowledge systems fail within a month. Not because people are undisciplined — because the systems are designed wrong. They demand constant decisions, upfront commitment, and ongoing maintenance. The exact things that disappear when life gets busy.

My Portable Brain is built around a different set of assumptions.

---

## The three failure modes of PKM

**Failure mode 1: Too much friction at capture.**
If putting something into your system requires deciding where it goes, you'll stop capturing. The decision cost is too high in the moment. You were mid-conversation, mid-shower, mid-thought. You'll "do it later." You won't.

The fix: one inbox. Everything lands there. Zero decisions at capture time.

**Failure mode 2: Structure that doesn't match reality.**
Most systems are designed around an idealized version of how people work. Forty-seven folders, elaborate tag taxonomies, two-hour weekly reviews. Real people have jobs, families, energy levels that vary, and weeks that go sideways.

The fix: a system opinionated enough to have a shape, flexible enough to bend without breaking. PARA — four categories, not forty.

**Failure mode 3: No memory.**
You set up a beautiful system, use it for three weeks, something interrupts the habit, and when you come back you can't remember how it works. You restart. This is the doom loop that kills most systems.

The fix: an agent that holds the context. The vault doesn't depend on your memory of how it works. You can disappear for three weeks and come back to a system that still knows where things are.

---

## The design principles

**Markdown as source of truth.**
Every note, template, and agent instruction is a plain text file. No app lock-in. No proprietary format. Open in any editor. Version-control with git. Readable on any device. The files outlast any tool.

**The agent is a collaborator, not an autocrat.**
It suggests, you decide. Never moves files without showing you first. Never adds commitments without asking. Never archives without confirmation. Its job is to reduce friction — not take over.

**Self-improvement through observation.**
The agent doesn't analyze your psychology. It watches for patterns in how you actually use the system. When it sees the same correction three times, it asks if you want to make it permanent. One suggestion per session, maximum. No unsolicited insights. Just a system that gets more calibrated to you over time.

**Progressive complexity.**
Useful on day one with almost nothing in it. Gets more useful as you use it — not because you've done setup work, but because the agent has learned your patterns and the vault has accumulated your actual knowledge.

---

## What this is not

Not a "second brain" in the sense of storing everything you've ever read. It's a working system organized around what you're doing, what you're responsible for, and what matters to you. The goal is clarity and reduced friction — not completeness.

Not automated in the sense that decisions get made for you. The agent handles logistics. You handle judgment.

---

## The one thing

The inbox rule. Everything starts there. If you do only one thing consistently, the rest of the vault stays healthy almost automatically.

Everything else is in service of that habit.
